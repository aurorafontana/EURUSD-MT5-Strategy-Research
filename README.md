# EURUSD MT5 Strategy Research

> Open research project for the systematic analysis and improvement of a Python/MetaTrader 5 EURUSD trading strategy.

## Status

**Experimental / research only. Not ready for live trading.**

This repository documents an ongoing forward-testing project based on the open-source **Ryuryu's FOREX MT5 EURUSD Bot** by Ryan Hayabusa (`ryu878/MT5-python-bot`). The original strategy is a short-only EURUSD strategy running on the M1 timeframe. This research project preserves the original idea as a baseline while testing controlled variations in trading hours, direction, entry filters, take profit, stop loss, and position/basket management.

The central research problem is simple but important:

> **The strategy can produce a high percentage of winning trades, but a relatively small number of large losing trades can erase the accumulated profits. Can risk and basket management be improved without destroying the entry logic that produces the high win rate?**

The purpose of making this project public is to invite developers, quantitative traders, data analysts, and MetaTrader/Python researchers to review the methodology, challenge assumptions, reproduce the analysis, and propose measurable improvements.

## Key Research Snapshot

A historical trade-path study covering **1 June to 16 July 2026** analyzed **1,823 closed trades** against EURUSD M1 market data.

| Metric | Result |
|---|---:|
| Closed trades analyzed | 1,823 |
| Winning trades | 1,512 |
| Losing trades | 311 |
| Win rate | 82.94% |
| Gross profit | +€3,883.53 |
| Gross loss | -€5,581.09 |
| Net result | -€1,697.56 |
| Profit factor | 0.696 |

These results summarize a research period containing multiple experimental variants and should **not** be interpreted as the performance of one homogeneous production strategy.

### MAE of winning trades

Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE) was reconstructed for the winning trades using EURUSD M1 history.

| Statistic | MT5 points | Pips |
|---|---:|---:|
| Mean | 61 | 6.1 |
| Median | 36 | 3.6 |
| 75th percentile | 83 | 8.3 |
| 90th percentile | 148 | 14.8 |
| 95th percentile | 202 | 20.2 |
| 99th percentile | 242 | 24.2 |

This creates an important optimization problem. A tighter stop can reduce catastrophic losses, but if it is too tight it also converts trades that historically recovered into losing trades.

A static historical simulation found that a stop around **245 points (24.5 pips)** preserved approximately **99.21% of historically winning trades** in the analyzed sample, while tighter stops increasingly removed eventual winners. This result is a hypothesis for forward testing, not proof of an optimal stop.

- Platform: MetaTrader 5
- Language: Python
- Primary timeframe: M1

## The Core Problem

The strategy exhibits an asymmetric payoff structure:

1. Many trades close with relatively small profits.
2. The win rate can remain high for extended periods.
3. Additional positions may accumulate during adverse price movement.
4. A persistent directional move can create a large losing basket.
5. A few full stop losses can erase profits generated by many winners.

Therefore, optimizing only for **win rate** is not sufficient.

The research focuses on:

- expectancy;
- profit factor;
- maximum drawdown;
- MAE and MFE;
- loss distribution;
- basket exposure;
- FIRST vs ADD entries;
- time in trade;
- trading-hour effects;
- market regime filters;
- volatility at entry;
- trend strength;
- recovery behavior;
- risk-adjusted returns.

## Current Research Direction

The project is moving toward a smaller number of clearly isolated tests rather than running many overlapping variants.

The main baseline under investigation uses parameters around:

- EURUSD
- M1 primary execution timeframe
- 0.10 lot research size in demo testing
- controlled trading hours
- maximum concurrent-position limits
- TP restored to the original 21-point research baseline for controlled comparisons
- original wide SL used as a control
- separate Magic Numbers for experiment attribution
- detailed trade-entry logging

The next major comparison is expected to isolate **risk management** while keeping entry conditions unchanged.

Example hypothesis:

- Control: TP 21 / SL 273
- Candidate: TP 21 / SL 245

The purpose is to determine whether the candidate improves expectancy and drawdown in forward testing without materially damaging the recovery behavior responsible for the high historical win rate.

## Why Static Stop-Loss Simulation Is Not Enough

Changing the stop loss changes the future state of the trading system.

If a position closes earlier:

- exposure becomes available sooner;
- a new FIRST entry may become possible;
- an ADD entry may never occur;
- the composition of a basket changes;
- subsequent trades can differ from historical trades.

For this reason, historical MAE analysis is useful for **generating hypotheses**, but the final evaluation requires event-driven backtesting or controlled forward testing that reconstructs the strategy state dynamically.

This is one of the areas where community contributions would be particularly valuable.

## Logging and Data Collection

Early versions of detailed indicator logging generated extremely large files (approximately 200 MB in a single trading day). Logging was therefore redesigned to focus on trade events rather than every market tick.

The research logging goal is to capture, at minimum, for every entry:

- timestamp;
- symbol;
- Magic Number / experiment ID;
- FIRST or ADD classification;
- BUY or SELL;
- entry price;
- lot size;
- TP and SL;
- spread;
- SMA values used by the strategy;
- RSI M1/M5/M15 where relevant;
- ATR / volatility context;
- ADX and directional indicators where tested;
- distance from relevant moving averages;
- number of open positions in the basket;
- basket average price;
- current basket floating P/L;
- session / hour;
- eventual exit reason;
- realized P/L;
- MAE;
- MFE;
- time in trade.

The objective is not to add indicators indiscriminately. The objective is to create enough context to compare winning and losing trades and identify statistically meaningful differences.

## Research Questions — Help Wanted

We welcome evidence-based discussion and reproducible contributions around these questions:

1. Can the stop loss be reduced without materially destroying the strategy's recovery-based win rate?
2. Should risk be managed per position or at basket level?
3. How should additional entries be limited during persistent trends?
4. Can ATR or realized volatility produce a more robust dynamic stop than a fixed SL?
5. Which indicators can distinguish mean-reversion conditions from trend continuation without excessive lag?
6. Do ADX/DI, moving-average slope, RSI, ATR, or multi-timeframe structure help filter dangerous ADD entries?
7. Should FIRST and ADD entries use different risk rules?
8. Is there a statistically significant difference between morning, midday, afternoon, and Friday performance?
9. Should new entries be restricted late on Friday to avoid carrying vulnerable baskets across the weekend?
10. How should MFE be used to test whether TP 21 leaves too much favorable excursion uncaptured?
11. Can partial exits or trailing logic improve expectancy without reducing the high hit rate excessively?
12. How can we build an event-driven backtester that reproduces the Python/MT5 execution logic accurately?
13. How should spread, slippage, commissions, swaps, latency, and broker execution be modeled?
14. How can we avoid overfitting while iterating on a relatively small sample?
15. What walk-forward and out-of-sample validation framework would be most appropriate?

If you have experience with MetaTrader 5, Python, systematic trading, quantitative research, or time-series analysis, contributions are welcome.

## Reproducibility Principles

Every proposed strategy change should ideally follow this process:

1. State the hypothesis before testing.
2. Change one major variable at a time whenever possible.
3. Assign a unique experiment ID / Magic Number.
4. Record the exact code version.
5. Record the exact test period.
6. Preserve raw data separately from derived analysis.
7. Report wins and losses, not only win rate.
8. Include spread, commission, swap, and slippage assumptions.
9. Separate in-sample research from out-of-sample validation.
10. Forward-test promising changes in demo before considering live use.

## Important Limitations

The available research results currently combine periods in which different versions and experimental configurations were active. Consequently:

- aggregate historical performance is not a clean backtest of one fixed strategy;
- overlapping A/B tests may create correlated exposure;
- historical simulations may not reproduce dynamic re-entry behavior;
- demo execution does not guarantee live execution quality;
- results may be broker-specific;
- sample sizes for some variants are small;
- no claim of profitability is made.

A major goal of the repository is to improve this experimental methodology.
